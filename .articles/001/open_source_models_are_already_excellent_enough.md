# Open-source models are already excellent enough — I see no reason to keep paying for paid models

I used Claude Sonnet 4.5 long enough to extract a certain standard from it. In mathematics, it helped me better understand the material through more didactic explanations, helped me redo solutions, and review my line of reasoning. In programming and documentation, it served to structure files, rewrite passages, and suggest ways to organize material. The experience was good. Precisely because of that, it ended up having a consequence that may seem counterintuitive: after understanding the level of quality I needed, it became easier to notice when other, much cheaper models were already delivering what was necessary.

The name of the model, whether the model comes from a big tech company in commercial LLMs, whether it is paid or not, stopped being the focus of this line of reasoning of mine. What truly started to matter was something else: whether it can keep up with my study and work flow. This flow involves studying mathematics, reading material, writing reports, organizing directories, reviewing files, checking consistency between sections, programming, testing, execution instructions, and documentation. It is not a use case in which I ask half a dozen questions and judge whether the answer was pleasant to me or not. In this type of use, a low-cost model that can withstand multiple passes can produce more value for the user than a more expensive model used with caution.

That was the central shift in my use of AI: I stopped worrying about using paid models — with absurd prompt engineering to save tokens. Adding credits to OpenRouter and subscribing to Ollama Cloud make more sense for my current moment.

That said, there is a detail in what I just said that I would like to emphasize — and that many people foolishly underestimate: caring about the price per million tokens completely changes the way you study and work. When each session with a top commercial LLM leads the user to constantly apply token-control strategies, the quality of the final result is often sacrificed. This is something that does not happen today when we use open-source models, with drastically cheaper prices, which makes the user's study and work flow, as it has been for me, much more effective and productive.

A few weeks ago, I conducted research using Qwen3.5 Plus via the OpenRouter API inside Codex CLI. The cost was around US$25. In my work, this model helped organize the working directory, separate the main report from auxiliary documents, structure notes, standardize Markdown, review coherence between sections, among several other activities that previously would only have been performed with relative perfection by a commercial model.

Another point: the cost comparison impressed me. Sonnet 4.5 was in the range of US$3 per million input tokens and US$15 per million output tokens. Qwen3.5 Plus was close to US$0.26 per million input tokens and US$1.56 per million output tokens. The research that cost around US$25 could, with a similar volume, have reached the range of a few hundred dollars using Sonnet 4.5 — considering a reasonable estimate, something around US$240 to US$290.

Then came another case.

In a later research project, I used DeepSeek V4 Flash via the OpenRouter API in Codex. The approximate cost was an incredible US$5. The model's role was similar to that of the previously mentioned Qwen model. US$5 for this type of flow truly changes the way the user performs his work. DeepSeek V4 Flash was in the range of US$0.098 per million input tokens and US$0.196 per million output tokens. With Sonnet 4.5, a similar run could easily reach hundreds of dollars, depending on the token ratio. In a flow with a lot of input, something like US$175 to US$250 would be a plausible estimate. The point is that, for US$5, I was able to use the model as a work tool without worrying about my OpenRouter credits running out quickly.

I usually compare this to a marathon runner: the last thing he wants to worry about is his shoe becoming loose on his foot in the middle of the race. To perform calmly and at his maximum potential, the shoe needs to provide security and comfort.

Before, it was easy to associate quality with the most expensive model. Today, that association seems outdated to me. When it comes to using LLMs for studying, a mathematics task may require patience and clarity; when it comes to work, a documentation task may require dozens of small revisions, a programming task may require reading files, testing, adjusting, and explaining, just as a research project may require organizing material before it even requires more ambitious formulations. Placing all of this under a single subscription to a commercial LLM is comfortable, but not necessarily intelligent. In many moments, the best model for the job is not the one that wins a benchmark, but the one that offers the best cost-benefit ratio.

What I mean is this: according to my usage, a US$20 Ollama Cloud subscription combined with US$30 monthly credits on OpenRouter results in absurd peace of mind when it comes to usage limits — yes, the great villain of a creative person. No, this decision does not lower the quality of my work. On the contrary — it increases the amount of verifiable work I can do with the same budget. I get more revision capacity, more checking, more ability to keep an agent working on a directory without having to interrupt the process to save tokens.

For me, open-source models do not need to be superior at everything. They only need to solve what I actually do: study and work. Qwen3.5 Plus and DeepSeek V4 Flash demonstrated this masterfully in practice.
